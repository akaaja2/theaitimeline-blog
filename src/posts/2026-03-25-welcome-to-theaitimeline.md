Artificial intelligence is moving fast. New models ship weekly. Benchmarks get broken and re-broken. Startups raise enormous rounds, get acquired, or quietly disappear. Keeping track of what actually matters — and what's just noise — has become its own full-time job.

TheAITimeline exists to make that easier.

## What this site covers

Every post here is about one thing: what's happening in AI, clearly explained.

That means:

- **Model releases and updates** — what changed, what it means in practice, how it compares to what came before
- **Research developments** — key papers explained without the jargon, focused on real-world implications
- **Tools and productivity** — what's useful, what's overhyped, and how people are actually using AI to get work done
- **Industry moves** — acquisitions, funding rounds, and org changes that shape where the technology is headed

## What this site does not do

TheAITimeline does not editorialize. You won't find predictions, hot takes, or opinion pieces dressed up as news. The goal is to give you accurate, well-sourced information and let you draw your own conclusions.

There are plenty of places to read about how AI will either save or end civilization. This is not one of them.

## How posts are structured

Each post is focused on a single development or topic. Posts are short by design — typically 300 to 500 words — because your time matters. If a topic warrants deeper coverage, it gets its own extended piece, clearly labeled.

All posts include:
- A one-paragraph summary at the top
- Context for why it matters
- Links to primary sources where available

## A note on pace

New posts go up as events warrant — not on a fixed schedule. AI moves fast, but not every week produces something genuinely worth writing about. The goal is signal, not volume.

If you want to stay current, subscribe to the RSS feed at [/feed.xml](/feed.xml) or check back whenever something big happens. You'll know when that is.
