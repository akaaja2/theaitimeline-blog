The end of March brings a notable product shutdown, a new internet traffic milestone, and an escalating fight over who gets to regulate AI.

## OpenAI Shuts Down Sora

OpenAI announced the shutdown of its Sora video generation app and API on March 24, less than six months after launch. The app never gained traction: worldwide users peaked near one million then fell below 500,000, while the compute cost ran roughly $1 million per day.

The entertainment implications were sharp. Disney had committed $1 billion to a partnership with Sora; the company reportedly learned of the shutdown less than an hour before the public announcement, and the deal collapsed with it.

OpenAI said the underlying Sora technology — built to learn about physics and real-world motion — is being redirected toward robotics and physical-world applications rather than media generation. Sources: [VentureBeat](https://venturebeat.com/technology/openai-is-shutting-down-sora-its-powerful-ai-video-app), [Bloomberg](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-24/openai-plans-to-discontinue-support-for-sora-ai-video-generator), [TechCrunch](https://techcrunch.com/2026/03/29/why-openai-really-shut-down-sora/)

## AI Bots Now Outnumber Humans on the Internet

A report from cybersecurity firm Human Security found that automated AI traffic officially eclipsed human activity in 2025, growing nearly eight times faster than human-generated traffic. Traffic from AI agents like OpenClaw grew roughly 8,000% year over year.

The pattern is practical rather than abstract: when an AI agent performs a task such as comparison shopping, it may visit a thousand times as many pages as a human would for the same job. Cloudflare's CEO separately projected that bot traffic will exceed human traffic by 2027. Source: [CNBC](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/03/26/ai-bots-humans-internet.html)

## Federal vs. State AI Regulation

The Trump administration is pushing Congress to enact a unified federal AI regulatory framework — one that would preempt a growing patchwork of state laws. White House OSTP head Michael Kratsios argued that only Congress can provide the "certainty" innovators need.

States aren't waiting. NPR reported on March 28 that state legislatures have already passed dozens of AI laws covering child safety, algorithmic transparency, and whistleblower protections — and many state officials are openly skeptical that a gridlocked Congress will act. The tension is deepening as the White House's proposed framework moves through committee. Sources: [NPR](https://www.npr.org/2026/03/28/nx-s1-5755062/trump-wants-a-deadlocked-congress-to-move-on-ai-frustrated-states-say-they-already-have), [TechCrunch](https://techcrunch.com/2026/03/20/trumps-ai-framework-targets-state-laws-shifts-child-safety-burden-to-parents/)

## Tenex Raises $250M for AI Cybersecurity

Tenex.ai, a Google-partnered AI security startup, raised $250 million at a valuation above $1 billion on March 31. The round was led by Crosspoint Capital with participation from Shield Capital and DeepWork Capital. The company builds tools that use AI to detect and respond to cyberattacks, a segment drawing increased investor attention as AI-capable threat actors multiply. Source: [Bloomberg](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-31/google-partner-tenex-raises-250-million-for-ai-security-tools)

## AI Data Centers and Fossil Fuel Emissions

A study by the Rhodium Group attributed part of a 2.4% rise in U.S. fossil fuel emissions last year to surging AI data center power demand. The finding complicates sustainability commitments from major cloud providers, many of whom had previously announced clean energy targets that AI infrastructure spending is now straining. Source: [Fortune](https://fortune.com/2026/03/29/big-tech-climate-change-goals-data-centers-ai-fossil-fuels/)
